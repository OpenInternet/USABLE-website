Reducing Information Poverty

Access, Inclusion, Content, and Engagement

Internews’ mission is to reduce “information poverty” We approach that through the lenses of removing barriers to access, addressing systematic exclusion, supporting the creation of content and enabling full engagement with that content.

What does Access to Information mean?

Access to information is not binary - it involves the costs associated with that access.

Access to information is not binary - it also involves costs, financial, social, and even safety related. In our Global Technology division, my team’s focus is on the safety and privacy aspects -- is content being censored? Are people at risk for what that say or do online?
This can get very technical, and very dangerous; whether you’re a journalist publishing news critical of your government, an LGBTI advocate, or documenting human rights abuses; the simple act of collecting and sharing information can lead to very real repercussions.

Usability and Accessibility

Usable Security Apps By Leveraging End Users

This led us to launching USABLE, which brings the developers of tools built for safety to marginalized communities who could benefit the most from them, and facilitate a conversation about what threats these communities face, and what barriers there are in adopting tools and practices to respond.

The Challenge

Inclusion and Accessibility

We worked with groups including election monitors, women journalists, and LGBTI activists, but the most exceptional group was a consortia of disability rights advocates in Mexico.
Let’s think for a moment about a person with visual impairments - if apps don’t have sensible labels assigned to the interfaces of their element, these tools become absolutely useless. What we might see as “Accept” and “Cancel” could be read out to someone using a screen reader as “Button 1” and “Button 2” - or the feared "hamburger button".
And let's not even start with the complexities faced when using screen readers combined with translated interfaces

It's not just sight

Hearing, physical and mobility, and cognitive or nervous system disorders all impact your ability to engage and present barriers for new technologies.
Mobile banking -- especially for people who live far from branches -- is amazingly useful and empowering, but unless the tools are developed with PwDs in mind, they will be even further excluded.

Solutions

The flip side of all of this is that technology - and especially mobile -restore incredible amounts of autonomy to daily life, in everything from apps to help ensure clothes match to apps that use machine learning to identify objects to the simple value of turn by turn map directions.
Mobile in particular, with built-in screen reading, offer incredible potential to reduce inequalities faced by PwDs.

Empathy matters.

Building feedback loops and real-world connections to marginalized communities is key to effective programs.

Human Centered Design is as valid in program design as app development

We have to make sure we are designing with beneficiaries, and not simply for them, and this is as important in project design as in app design. This requires thoughtful and respectful engagement - both with the community and with program staff and technical developers.
At it's core, this is simply "human centered design"

Engagement is … Educational.


Developing with communities and with diverse teams creates better and more usable tools, this goes for PwDs as well as marginalized and at-risk community. Designing for the extremes at the outset.
We are expanding our USABLE work this year to double down on accessibility in the digital safety and security space, as accessibility tools are not necessarily made with security in mind, and vice versa, which is why building connections between users and developers is so vital.
